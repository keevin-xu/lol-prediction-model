Part 1

T1 model overview and the real use case

This section reruns the actual production model — the V2 walk-forward ELO tracker (K=32, blend_k=5, scale=400, half_life=270d, mov_weight=1.5, no soloq, identity calibration) — directly against the LCK/LPL/LCS match history in the database. These numbers are re-derived in this session, not copied from CLAUDE.md, and they match it closely (small differences are rounding/window artifacts) — which is itself useful confirmation that the documented numbers are reproducible.

Where the edge comes from

Same mechanism as T2: the model is not exceptional (62–68% depending on league). It beats the Polymarket opening price, which on T1 markets is even softer than T2's because the market opens six days before the match — far longer before sharp money arrives. The edge is a timing exploit, not a forecasting one.

Part 2 — The Centerpiece

Fine-grained entry-timing backtest

The CLAUDE.md timing table sampled 6 points (open, 1h, 4h, 24h, 48h, 4d) and used a Monte Carlo simulator with an assumed model accuracy — not the real model. This rerun uses the actual V2 model's walk-forward predictions against the actual quoted price at 10 points between market open and 24 hours later, resampled directly from each market's real 10-minute-resolution CLOB price history (250 markets, 26–4,483 price points each). It also recomputes the original hours-before-match grid for direct comparison.

Headline finding: the profitable window is real but narrower and noisier than the coarse grid implied

ROI per bet (the number that actually matters — it bakes in win/loss outcomes and the T1 cost model, not just price drift) is statistically indistinguishable from "strongly positive" through roughly the first 3–6 hours after market open. The 95% bootstrap CI starts crossing zero around +6h. By +24h, the point estimate is already negative (–5.4%, CI [–30%, +22%]) — earlier than the CLAUDE.md note that CLV "stays near zero" at 24h. The hours-before-match grid (which samples the tail end of the same 6-day window) is unambiguous: every single point from 24h-before-match through 1h-before-match shows negative ROI per bet. There is no late-entry sweet spot in this window — none of the 8 tested pre-match points clear zero.

CLV and hit rate disagree past +6h — that's the real warning sign

Mean CLV stays positive all the way to +24h (+0.083) — the line keeps drifting toward the model's side on average. But hit rate falls from 63% at open to 38.9% at +24h, well below the model's own ~62–68% raw accuracy. This is consistent with adverse selection: trades where the model still disagrees with the market by >10% after 24 hours of correction are disproportionately the cases where the market was right and the model was wrong, not cases of slow-moving sharp money. CLV alone would have made +24h look fine. ROI/bet (which is decided by actual outcomes, not price drift) shows it isn't.

Where the edge crosses zero — and where the real tradeoff sits

Reading the forward-from-open curve plainly: ROI/bet is solidly positive and overlapping in confidence interval from open through about +3h (point estimates +27% to +39%, all CIs clearing zero). The apparent peak at +1h/+2h is not a confirmed sweet spot — the CIs at open, +1h, +2h, and +3h all overlap heavily, so this is most consistent with one flat high-edge plateau, not a precise timing optimum. From +6h to +18h, point estimates stay positive but the CI straddles zero — not distinguishable from break-even at this sample size. By +24h the point estimate goes negative, and every hours-before-match point tested (1h to 24h before the match) is negative. There is no rescue point later in the window.

The real tradeoff: edge vs. depth, and depth is still unmeasured

Market open has the most edge and is the truncated end of the curve. The thinnest order book is also most likely at that exact moment — a market six days from a match has had zero time to accumulate liquidity. If real depth at open turns out to be near zero, the highest-edge window may also be the least fillable one. The next 3–6 hours after open are the only other zone where the CI clears zero, and structurally probably have more accumulated depth than the instant of creation — but how much more is exactly the unmeasured quantity. Any claim of a "sweet spot" between open and +6h is conditional on depth observation confirming fillable size holds up in that window — this has not been measured. Section 3's depth-sensitivity sweep quantifies how much that matters.

Read this ROI with care

+126% ROI is identical in percentage terms from $1K to $50K because the 2% per-market cap — not depth — is the binding constraint at every level tested (cap-bound on 100% of trades). That means this backtest cannot answer "does T1 capacity beat T2's $5–10K ceiling?" — it shows what happens if the cap, not the market, is always what limits you. The market-imposed limit is exactly the thing that hasn't been measured.

What this actually says about the $50K+ capacity case

At $10K bankroll, the strategy is fairly robust to depth being wrong — ROI only meaningfully erodes once real fillable drops below ~10% of the proxy (≈$800/market). At $50K, it's much more fragile: depth starts binding noticeably even at 50% of the proxy, and by 10% of the proxy (≈$820/market, the same threshold that barely dents the $10K case) ROI at $50K has already fallen from +126% to +61% — roughly half the headline return. The larger the bankroll, the more the entire case rests on a number that has never been measured. A bankroll above ~$10–25K should be treated as contingent on direct observation of opening-hour order book depth, not on this backtest.

What this means for the open question

If real opening depth is…Then…
≥$1,500–2,000/market (20%+ of proxy)$50K+ bankroll case holds roughly as backtested; T1 capacity meaningfully exceeds T2
≈$500–800/market (5–10% of proxy)$10K bankroll still works well; above that, returns degrade materially — somewhere in this range a capacity ceiling similar to T2's emerges for T1
≤$150–200/market (≤2% of proxy)Depth binds even at $10K; T1's capacity advantage over T2 evaporates regardless of edge size

This is exactly the table that should be filled in by the passive depth-observation work described in CLAUDE.md — it is currently a scenario analysis, not a measurement.

What changed vs. the existing CLAUDE.md T1 numbers

CLAUDE.md's T1 P&L figures (162 trades, +0.160 mean CLV, 64.2% hit rate) come from backtest/t1_backtest.py, a Monte Carlo simulator that assumes a model accuracy parameter and assigns a fixed 0.65 probability to the model's "correct" side — it does not run the actual ELO model. This report's numbers come from running the actual model. The per-league accuracy figures match almost exactly (confirming those are reproducible); the open-market trade count (153 vs. 162) and CLV (+0.169 vs. +0.160) are close but not identical, likely due to differences in team-name matching coverage. The timing-decay shape is directionally consistent with CLAUDE.md (edge decays, eventually goes negative) but the fine-grained version places the zero-crossing earlier (~+6h to +24h after open) than the coarse version implied (CLAUDE.md's coarsest sample point in this window was 24h, reported as "+0.008, nearly zero" — this rerun's +24h point is already at –5.4% ROI/bet).

Known limitations of this rerun

Team-name matching dropped 28/296 markets (11 + 17 unmatched team names) and the region/min-games gates dropped a further 22 — consistent with, but not identical to, the gates used elsewhere in this codebase. The depth proxy (fillable as % of lifetime volume) is unchanged from the existing T1 cost model and remains the single largest unvalidated assumption in every number in Part 3. Bootstrap CIs use 5,000 resamples per timing point; with overlapping underlying markets across adjacent time points, timing-point estimates are correlated with each other and should be read as one continuous curve, not independent tests.
