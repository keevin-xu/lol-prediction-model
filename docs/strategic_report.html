Section 1

The edge is a closing window

The model is not exceptional (~62–64% accuracy). The Polymarket opening line is worse — only 54% accurate on Tier 2 matches. The strategy bets the gap before sharp money corrects the line. That window closes within hours of market open. Every metric below follows from this single fact.

The strategic thesis

A 63% model doesn't beat an efficient market. It beats a 54% opening line that hasn't been corrected yet. By 2–4 hours before match time, the line is already 66–67% accurate and the model loses money betting against it. The edge is in when you bet, not how good the model is.

The model accuracy is good-not-great on purpose. A 63% model that clears a 54% bar beats a theoretically superior model that can't be deployed because the target market is already efficient. Lower model risk is a feature: if ELO degrades 2%, the strategy survives. If the timing window closes, the strategy dies — and that's the real risk to monitor.

Section 2

Prediction accuracy

All accuracy numbers are walk-forward — only historical matches before each prediction date are used to build ELOs. There is no data leakage. A prior version with lookahead bias showed 65–72% accuracy; the true walk-forward number is 63–64%.

Walk-forward discipline

An earlier backtest used final (fully-trained) ELOs to predict past matches, inflating hit rate from 65% to 72% and ROI from +25% to +40%. This was caught and fixed in June 2026. All numbers below are from the corrected, no-lookahead pipeline.

What the model uses

V2 ELO: K=32, margin-of-victory scaling (mov_weight=1.5), 270-day half-life decay, regional initialization offsets. No soloq, no draft features, no roster signals — all tested and either null or counterproductive. Identity calibration (a=1.0, b=0.0): the model's raw logistic output is already well-calibrated, and Platt scaling added nothing on out-of-sample betting data.

Accuracy ceiling

The V3 model (107 features: dragon rates, baron rates, champion stats, vision, draft quality) gave 0% accuracy gain over V1 ELO. The ceiling from publicly available pre-match data appears to be ~64%. This was tested twice, with two separate approaches, and is not a computation error. Future model improvements need a different data source, not more features from the same source.

Section 4

What would have to be true to scale

Three constraints determine the ceiling. Two are resolved in principle; one is not. The manager's most useful focus is on the third.

Constraint 1 — Cost model validation (T2 and T1)

The estimate_opening_cost and estimate_fillable_at_open functions have never been checked against a real fill. Paper trading is live; live trading is not. Every P&L number in this report assumes simulated fills at modeled costs.

Resolves by: Forward paper trading with fill logging (predicted vs. actual slippage on every bet). The current paper trader does not log this. It must be added before the cost model can be trusted. 20+ fills are needed to know whether the cost estimate is within 1× or 2×.

Why it matters: The gap between 1× and 2× costs on T2 is $597 on a $1K bankroll and ~$3,000 on a $5K bankroll. On T1, it's larger. This is the single biggest unresolved risk.

Constraint 2 — T2 capacity ceiling (~$5–10K bankroll)
T2 market depth is thin: $15K median total volume, $20–$300 fillable at open per bet. Quarter-Kelly sizing at $10K bankroll means $625 max per bet; most markets can't absorb that. Above $10K, increasing Kelly fraction barely helps because depth runs out first.

T2 is viable at $1–5K bankroll. Above $10K, you are constrained by depth, not strategy quality. The capacity ceiling is not a modeling failure — it's a structural feature of thin T2 markets. The only paths past it are: (a) more T2 leagues with similar characteristics, or (b) T1.

Constraint 3 — T1 opening-hour depth (the blocking question)

T1 markets (LCK, LPL, LCS) open 6 days before the match. The model's edge lives entirely in the first hours after market creation — by 24 hours later, the CLV is near zero.

What the manager should push on

The cost model is the weakest link. The P&L range (+51% to +148%) is not a confidence interval — it's an uncertainty range over a single unknown that has been modeled but never measured. Logging actual fill slippage on paper trades is the highest-value near-term action. Everything else (T1 expansion, Kelly upgrade, bankroll scaling) is downstream of that validation.
